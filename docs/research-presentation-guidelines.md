# Research Presentation Guidelines for Pause & Flourish

## Overview

Integrating peer-reviewed research into Pause & Flourish will significantly elevate the site's authority and trust. This document outlines the best practices for formatting, citing, and presenting academic literature to a consumer audience, based on industry leaders like Healthline, Examine.com, and Verywell Health.

## 1. Citation Format Standard

For a health and wellness site, the **NLM (National Library of Medicine) / Vancouver style** is the industry standard [1]. It is concise, professional, and easily understood by medical reviewers.

### Format Structure
**Author(s). Title. Journal Name. Year;Volume(Issue):Pages. doi:xxx**

### Examples
* **Standard Journal Article:** Liu Y, Guo Z. Dietary interventions and nutritional strategies for menopausal health: a mini review. *Front Nutr*. 2025;12:1702105. doi:10.3389/fnut.2025.1702105
* **Cochrane Review:** Rodriguez MB, et al. Long-term hormone therapy for perimenopausal and postmenopausal women. *Cochrane Database Syst Rev*. 2025. PMID:41307293

## 2. In-Article Presentation

When referencing research within articles or product reviews, follow a two-tier approach to balance readability with academic rigor.

### Inline Citations
Use superscript numbers linked directly to the source (e.g., PubMed or DOI link) [2]. Avoid interrupting the reading flow with full author names or dates unless the specific study is the focal point of the paragraph.

> **Example:** "A recent review of dietary interventions suggests that Mediterranean diet adherence can significantly reduce vasomotor symptoms in perimenopausal women [1]."

### Reference Lists
Include a complete, numbered reference list at the bottom of the article, formatted in NLM style.

## 3. Dedicated Research Summaries (The "Research Feed" Model)

If Pause & Flourish develops a dedicated "Research" or "Clinical Insights" section (similar to Examine.com), each study should be presented as an individual "Article Card" [3].

### Article Card UI Components

| Component | Description | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Study Type Badge** | Colour-coded tag indicating the level of evidence | `[Meta-Analysis]` `[RCT]` `[Observational]` |
| **Consumer Headline** | A plain-language translation of the study title | "Mediterranean Diet Linked to Fewer Hot Flashes" |
| **Academic Citation** | The formal NLM citation | Liu Y, et al. *Front Nutr*. 2025. |
| **Key Takeaway** | A 2-3 sentence summary of the practical finding | "Women adhering to a Mediterranean diet experienced a 30% reduction in severe vasomotor symptoms compared to the control group." |
| **Source Link** | Direct link to the full text or abstract | `[View on PubMed]` |

## 4. Trust and Transparency Signals

To align with Google's E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) guidelines for YMYL (Your Money or Your Life) content [4], implement the following signals on all research-heavy pages:

* **Medical Review Badge:** "Medically reviewed by [Name, Credentials]" at the top of the article.
* **Last Updated Date:** Clearly display when the content was last reviewed against current literature.
* **Medical Disclaimer:** A persistent footer or header stating: *"Content is for informational and educational purposes only and is not intended as a substitute for professional medical advice."*

## 5. Recommended Research Sources for Menopause

When sourcing new studies for the site, prioritize the following high-impact journals and databases:

* **PubMed (NIH):** The primary database for biomedical literature.
* **Menopause (The Journal of The Menopause Society):** The leading journal for menopause-specific clinical research.
* **Cochrane Library:** The gold standard for systematic reviews and meta-analyses.
* **Frontiers in Reproductive Health:** Open-access journal frequently publishing modern menopause research.
